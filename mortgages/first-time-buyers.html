First-time buyer advice built around the UK lending reality

Most first-time buyers in the UK put down between 5% and 15% of the purchase price. A 5% deposit on a £250,000 flat is £12,500 — a 15% deposit on the same flat is £37,500, and that ten-percentage-point jump usually unlocks notably better rates. Knowing where your deposit sits in the LTV bands (95%, 90%, 85%, 80%) is often the single biggest lever on what you'll pay.

Alongside deposit, lenders assess affordability on income multiples (typically 4.5x gross annual income, sometimes 5.5x for higher earners) and stress-test the mortgage at a higher rate than the headline product. Credit history, existing commitments, childcare costs and recent overdraft use all feed into the final borrowing figure.

A short call confirms your realistic borrowing number, the right scheme (First Homes, Shared Ownership, standard 95% LTV, Guarantor), and which lenders will actually accept your profile before you start viewings.

The first-time buyer mortgage process

While every purchase is different, the process usually follows a few key stages.

Step 1

Affordability & DIP

15-minute call confirms income, deposit, credit and timeline. We place a Decision in Principle with the lender most likely to lend, usually within 24 hours.

Step 2

Offer agreed on a property

Once your offer is accepted, we submit the full application. Estate agents take buyers with a real DIP far more seriously than those without one.

Step 3

Valuation & formal offer

The lender instructs its valuation, underwrites the case and issues a formal mortgage offer — typically within two to four weeks of full submission.

Step 4

Exchange & completion

We stay in contact with you, the lender and your solicitor through exchange of contracts to completion — and confirm protection cover is in place before keys.

First-Time Buyer FAQs

Questions first-time buyers often ask

How much deposit do first-time buyers really need?

The UK market has genuine 95% LTV products, so 5% of the purchase price is the functional minimum. That said, the rate step-downs at 90%, 85% and 80% LTV are significant — adding even a few thousand more can materially reduce your monthly payment over a five-year fix.

How much can I actually borrow?

Most high-street lenders cap at around 4.5x gross annual income. A few will stretch to 5.5x for professional borrowers or higher earners. A dual-income household on £45k + £35k (£80k combined) could see lending offers ranging from £360k to £440k depending on commitments and credit profile.

Do first-time buyers pay Stamp Duty?

In England and Northern Ireland, first-time buyers currently pay no Stamp Duty on properties up to £425,000 and a reduced rate between £425,001 and £625,000. Scotland (LBTT) and Wales (LTT) have their own rules — we confirm the exact figure for your purchase before you budget.

What documents should I get ready now?

Photo ID, three months of payslips (or two years' SA302s and tax year overviews if self-employed), three months of current-account statements, proof of deposit source, and your most recent P60. Having these ready shortens the timeline from offer to mortgage offer by days, not hours.

What other costs should I budget for beyond the deposit?

Typical extras on a £250,000 purchase: solicitor fees £1,200–£1,800, survey £400–£800 (RICS Level 2) or £600–£1,200 (Level 3), mortgage valuation £0–£300 (often free), searches £300–£500, and removals £500–£1,500. Budget roughly 1.5–2% of purchase price for non-deposit costs.
