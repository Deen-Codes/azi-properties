Specialist Mortgage Guide

How can complex income affect mortgage options?

Two applicants with the same headline income can be offered very different loan sizes — because lenders don't all treat bonuses, dividends, day-rates or additional income streams the same way. Here's how to read the landscape.

"Complex income" simply means earnings that aren't a straight monthly salary. It's far more common than the name suggests — bonuses, commission, overtime, shift allowances, dividends, partnership profit, contract day-rates, rental income, pension drawdown, foreign income and trust distributions all fall into the category. Each lender has their own rules for how much of each income type they'll count, and those rules change the borrowing figure substantially.

1. Bonuses and commission

Most lenders accept some bonus or commission income, but they differ on:

  • The percentage they'll count — typically between 50% and 100%
  • Whether they need a one-year or two-year track record
  • Whether they'll use the latest year or an average of two or three years
  • How regular vs. one-off bonuses are treated

For someone with a large variable component — for example, a £70,000 base plus £40,000 typical bonus — the difference between a lender counting 50% of one year's bonus and another counting 100% averaged over two can be well over £100,000 of borrowing.

2. Overtime and shift allowances

Regular overtime and shift allowance is treated similarly to bonus — often counted at 50% to 100% depending on consistency. Emergency services, NHS and transport roles with predictable overtime patterns usually find lenders willing to count more of it, particularly where payslips show it as a recurring line.

3. Self-employed profits vs drawings

Self-employed sole traders are typically assessed on net profit shown on SA302s — not on the amount actually drawn from the business. This can work either way. If you drew less than the business earned, you get the benefit. If the business earned less than you personally took out (via prior-year profits), it may limit the borrowing.

4. Limited company directors: salary + dividend vs retained profit

This is one of the highest-impact areas for broker input. Most lenders assess limited company directors on salary plus dividends. A smaller but growing set of specialist lenders will also include the company's retained (undistributed) net profit — often called a "profit and salary" basis.

For directors who leave money in the business for tax efficiency, this one distinction can increase borrowing by tens or hundreds of thousands of pounds. Knowing which lenders work this way is often the single most valuable piece of advice for LTD company directors.

5. Contractors and day-rate workers

Many specialist lenders now assess contractors on a day-rate basis rather than through self-employed accounts:

  • Typical formula: daily rate × 5 days × 46–48 weeks
  • Requires a current signed contract and usually a 6–12 month track record
  • Often more generous than an accounts-based assessment

The catch: not every lender offers it. The right product often sits on a specialist panel rather than the high street.

6. Multiple income sources

If your income comes from several places — a PAYE job plus freelance work, two directorships, employment plus rental income — lenders vary in how they blend them. Some will count each source fully; others will only count the primary income and use secondary income at a reduced percentage. For applicants with diverse income, the right lender choice is often a bigger lever than the right rate.

7. Rental income from existing properties

If you already own rental property, the rental income can often be added to your affordability calculation — sometimes at 100%, sometimes net of the mortgage cost. Portfolio landlords with four or more mortgaged properties face additional scrutiny, usually with a stress-test across the whole portfolio.

8. Foreign currency income

Income paid in a currency other than sterling sits in a narrower lender pool. Lenders typically:

  • Apply a currency haircut (often 15% to 25%) to account for FX risk
  • Require evidence of the income source and how it's received
  • Limit LTV for foreign currency borrowers

9. Pension income and older borrowers

For applicants drawing from a pension, defined benefit schemes are generally treated more favourably than drawdown. Some lenders will use pension pot value in addition to income. Later-life lending has opened up considerably in recent years, but availability varies dramatically by lender.

10. Trust, investment and dividend income from third-party sources

Income from investments, trusts or family distributions is workable with the right lender, but usually needs a clear history and evidence of ongoing receipt. A private banking or high-net-worth desk is often the right route for these cases.

Why this matters in practice

Two applicants with identical paper earnings can receive quite different mortgage offers simply because one was placed with a lender whose rules matched their income shape. The difference between a standard high-street assessment and a specialist assessment is often the largest single factor in how much you can borrow.

Final thoughts

Complex income doesn't mean difficult. It means the lender choice matters more than it does for a straight PAYE case. A short, honest review of how each income component is paid — and how each lender treats it — usually surfaces the strongest option quickly.
