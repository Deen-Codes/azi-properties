Mortgage Affordability Guide

How much do I need to earn for a mortgage?

The amount you need to earn for a mortgage depends on your income, deposit, financial commitments and the lender’s affordability checks.

Many buyers want to know the exact salary needed to get a mortgage. The truth is there is no single income figure that applies to everyone.

Lenders usually look at your full financial picture, not just your salary. This means your deposit, spending, debts, credit profile and property plans can all influence what may be possible.

Why income matters

Income is one of the main factors lenders use when reviewing a mortgage application. It helps them understand how much borrowing may be affordable.

This can include salary, bonuses, overtime, commission, self-employed earnings or other income sources, depending on how the lender assesses them.

Affordability is not just salary

Even if two people earn the same amount, they may not be able to borrow the same amount. This is because affordability also looks at regular spending and existing financial commitments.

Credit cards, loans, car finance, childcare costs and other monthly commitments can all affect how much a lender may be willing to offer.

Deposit size can change the picture

Your deposit affects how much you need to borrow compared with the property value. A larger deposit may reduce the amount of borrowing needed and may open up different mortgage options.

However, deposit size does not replace affordability. Lenders still need to feel comfortable that repayments are manageable.

Property price matters too

The income needed for a mortgage will also depend on the property you want to buy. A higher property price usually means a larger mortgage, unless you are putting down a bigger deposit.

This is why it helps to look at property budget, deposit and income together rather than separately.

Self-employed income may be assessed differently

If you are self-employed, lenders may review your income differently from someone in regular employment. They may ask for accounts, tax calculations or other documents to understand your earnings.

This does not mean self-employed applicants cannot get a mortgage, but preparation and clear documentation can be especially important.

How can you prepare?

A good starting point is to understand your monthly income, current commitments, deposit savings and the price range of properties you are considering.

It can also help to review your credit profile, reduce unnecessary commitments where possible and gather documents early.

Final thoughts

The amount you need to earn for a mortgage depends on more than salary alone. Lenders usually assess income, affordability, commitments, deposit size and the property you want to buy.
