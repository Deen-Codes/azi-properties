Moving Home Guide

What should homeowners consider before moving?

The questions that matter most before a second move are almost always about clarity, not complexity. Work through these early and the move itself runs far more smoothly.

Moving home is rarely the same experience twice. A first-time buyer is juggling deposits and lenders. A second mover is juggling a sale, a purchase, an existing mortgage, a new one, and the timing between all of them. Most of the friction comes down to decisions that could have been made earlier.

1. Is your current mortgage portable?

Check what product you're on, when it ends, and what early repayment charge applies if you leave mid-term. Lenders differ on how flexibly they handle porting — some allow it with extra borrowing, some don't. This single check often decides the whole strategy.

2. What's your new borrowing going to look like?

Model the new mortgage at today's rates, not the rate you secured three or five years ago. A move "to a similar-sized home" can still mean a meaningfully higher monthly payment if your original rate was sub-2% and the market has moved on.

3. The total moving cost

It isn't just the deposit. A realistic second move includes:

  • Stamp duty (check the thresholds against your new purchase price)
  • Estate agent fees on the sale
  • Legal fees on both sides
  • Survey and valuation
  • Mortgage arrangement fees
  • Removals and storage
  • Any early repayment charge not covered by porting

Totalled up, these can run into tens of thousands of pounds. Knowing the number early often reshapes which properties are actually affordable.

4. Your timeline and chain

Are you buying first, selling first, or doing both simultaneously? Each approach has trade-offs. Chain-free buyers are attractive to sellers but may need bridging finance. Selling first removes pressure but usually means temporary accommodation. Decide which risk you're more willing to carry before you offer.

5. What's really driving the move?

The best decisions come from being honest about the "why". More space for a growing family is a different conversation from a lifestyle change or a commute adjustment. The right property, and the right mortgage structure, depend on knowing what you'd trade off for what.

6. Buildings insurance and exchange dates

Buildings cover on the new property is usually required at exchange — most lenders won't release funds without it. Make sure the policy is lined up early so it doesn't become a last-minute scramble. Buildings insurance is a homeownership essential rather than a mortgage product, so you can shop it around independently.

7. The right people in place early

A good solicitor, a broker you trust, and an estate agent who communicates are worth more than any single basis point on your mortgage rate. Most delays in a chain come from communication gaps, not legal complexity.

Final thoughts

The move that feels stressful is almost always the one where the decisions weren't made upfront. Work through the list above before you offer on the new place and the rest of the process becomes mechanical rather than messy.

Pre-move checklist

  • Current product & ERCs
  • New borrowing at today's rates
  • Total cost of moving
  • Timing & chain
  • Buildings insurance ready
