Residential vs buy-to-let mortgages: what's the difference?

Residential and buy-to-let mortgages are both secured on property, but almost everything about how they're assessed is different. Here's what investors and first-time landlords should understand before applying.

A residential mortgage is for a property you'll live in yourself. A buy-to-let mortgage is for a property you'll let out to tenants. The distinction sounds small, but it drives how lenders underwrite the loan, the deposit they'll want, and the rate you'll pay.

How lenders assess affordability

On a residential mortgage, lenders focus on your personal income, existing commitments and spending. On a buy-to-let mortgage, the assessment centres on the property's expected rental income — typically tested against a stress-tested interest rate. Personal income is usually a secondary consideration, though many lenders still set a minimum (commonly £25,000 for a single applicant).

That's why a high earner may struggle to secure a buy-to-let on a low-yielding property, while a lower earner with a strong rental property can often get approval.

Deposits and loan-to-value

Residential mortgages can go up to 95% loan-to-value (LTV) for owner-occupiers with a small deposit. Buy-to-let is different — most lenders cap LTV at around 75%, meaning a 25% deposit is typical. Specialist products may push higher, but pricing usually reflects the extra risk.

Interest rates and fees

Rates on buy-to-let tend to be higher than equivalent residential rates, and product fees are often larger — particularly on the headline "low rate" products. A 5-year fix with a £2,000 arrangement fee might work out worse than a slightly higher rate with no fee, depending on the loan size. The real cost is what matters, not the rate in isolation.

Repayment type

Most residential mortgages are repayment — you pay both interest and capital each month, so the balance reduces over time. Buy-to-let mortgages are often set up on an interest-only basis, which keeps monthly costs lower and preserves cash for other investments, but leaves the full loan balance to clear at the end of the term. How you plan to repay it matters.

Regulation

Most residential mortgages are regulated by the Financial Conduct Authority. Most standard buy-to-let mortgages fall outside FCA mortgage regulation, though "consumer buy-to-let" (for example, where you've inherited a property you never intended to let) is regulated. In practice, buy-to-let borrowers still benefit from working with regulated advisers.

Tax treatment

The tax treatment of buy-to-let property has shifted considerably in recent years. Mortgage interest on personally-held buy-to-lets is no longer fully deductible against rental income — relief is now given as a basic-rate tax credit. Many landlords now consider holding property through a limited company (an "SPV") for tax and succession reasons. Tax advice from a qualified accountant is usually worth the cost.

Which one fits your situation?

The decision is rarely close — it's driven by what you'll actually do with the property. A few common edge cases:

  • Let-to-buy — where you're letting out your current home to buy a new one
  • Consent to let — short-term permission from a residential lender to rent out a property
  • Accidental landlord — typically moving in with a partner and renting out an existing property

Final thoughts

Residential and buy-to-let mortgages solve different problems. Picking the wrong structure early on can cost time, tax and borrowing capacity later. A short whole-of-market review is usually enough to see which route fits.
