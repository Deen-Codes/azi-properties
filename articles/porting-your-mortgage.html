Moving Home Guide

Can you port your mortgage when moving home?

Porting lets you take your current mortgage deal to a new property instead of paying it off early. It often saves on early repayment charges — but it isn't automatic, and it doesn't always make sense.

A ported mortgage means you keep your existing rate and product with the same lender, moving the loan from your old property to your new one. You're still subject to a fresh application — but if approved, you avoid the early repayment charges (ERCs) you'd otherwise trigger by paying off the mortgage mid-term.

When porting usually makes sense

  • You're mid-way through a fixed or tracker deal with meaningful ERCs
  • Your rate is better than what's available in the current market
  • You're moving to a property of similar or higher value
  • Your income and circumstances haven't deteriorated

When porting usually doesn't make sense

  • Your current rate is worse than what's now available
  • ERCs are small or not applicable
  • You're downsizing and will reduce the loan significantly
  • A different lender offers a materially better package

What lenders reassess when you port

Porting isn't a formality. Most lenders reassess:

  • Affordability at current lending criteria (which may have tightened)
  • The property type and valuation
  • LTV on the new property
  • Credit profile since the original application

If you need to borrow more, the extra usually comes as a second "top-up" product at today's rates — meaning part of your mortgage sits on the old rate and part on the new one.

Timing: the back-to-back challenge

Some lenders require the sale and purchase to complete on the same day (a true port). Others allow a gap of up to a number of months — you pay off the mortgage on the sale, then apply for a refund of ERCs once the new property completes. If you're chain-free or bridging between moves, ask your lender's specific rules before committing.

The decision isn't just about ERCs

It's easy to focus on the early repayment charge and forget the rate itself. If you'll save £3,000 in ERCs by porting, but pay £8,000 extra in interest over the remaining fix because the new market rate is cheaper, porting is the more expensive route. The real comparison is total cost, not surface cost.

Alternatives if porting isn't right

  • Pay the ERC and switch to a better deal on the open market
  • Time the move to fall inside a product's ERC-free window
  • Negotiate with the lender — some will waive partial ERCs on porting

Final thoughts

Porting is a useful tool, but it's not the default answer. The right call depends on your current product, the new borrowing you need, the market's alternatives, and your lender's specific porting rules. A short review with a broker normally answers it definitively in a single call.
