Investor Guide

What should property investors consider before applying?

Before the mortgage application, there's a simpler set of questions every investor should be able to answer clearly. Here's the short list that tends to separate a strong application from a slow one.

Buy-to-let applications fail or stall for the same handful of reasons: unclear structure, marginal rental yield, a property that doesn't value up, or a deposit that can't be evidenced. Most of these can be prevented with a half-hour of preparation.

1. The rental yield and stress test

The rental income needs to cover the stressed interest on the loan, usually by 125% to 145% depending on tax status and rate type. Run the numbers before you make an offer:

  • Expected monthly rent (supported by local comparables)
  • Loan amount and the lender's stress rate
  • The coverage ratio at that rate

If the yield is borderline, you'll need either a larger deposit, a longer fixed-rate product (which often uses a lower stress rate), or a different property.

2. Personal name or limited company

Since the changes to mortgage interest relief, many landlords — particularly higher-rate taxpayers — hold properties inside a limited company (commonly called an SPV). It can reduce the tax bill on rental profits, but it introduces setup costs, ongoing accountancy and a narrower (though rapidly growing) lender panel. Speak to an accountant before you structure your first purchase.

3. Deposit source and evidence

Lenders need to see where the deposit has come from. If it's from savings, several months of bank statements are usually expected. If it's a gift, a signed gifted deposit letter. If it's from the sale of another property or a remortgage, a paper trail is required. Get this ready before the application — it's a common reason for delay.

4. Credit profile

Investors with a clean credit record and stable income will access the widest pricing. Recent missed payments, high credit utilisation or unresolved defaults narrow options — though specialist lenders exist for cases with blemishes. Pull your own credit file (via Experian, Equifax or TransUnion) before the first application.

5. Property type and lender appetite

Not every property fits every lender. Flats above shops, ex-local-authority blocks, HMOs, holiday lets and new-build apartments each attract a narrower pool. If you're targeting a specialist property type, talk to a broker first so you don't commit to a purchase you can't finance.

6. Existing portfolio and background affordability

If you already own other rental properties, most lenders will ask for a portfolio schedule and may stress-test the whole portfolio, not just the new purchase. Gearing (overall LTV across the portfolio) and background rental coverage both come into play.

7. Exit plan

If the mortgage is interest-only, lenders want to see a credible way the capital will be repaid — sale of the property, other savings, or a different asset. An exit plan isn't a formality: it shapes what products are available.

8. The unglamorous costs

Stamp duty (at the higher buy-to-let rate), legal fees, lender arrangement fees, valuation, broker fees, landlord insurance, void periods, letting agent fees, maintenance and Section 24 tax — all of these need to be modelled into the return. A property that looks like a 6% gross yield can quickly become a 3% net yield.

Final thoughts

A clear yield, a clean deposit trail, a credible structure and a property the lender can finance — get those four right and the mortgage is usually the easy part. A short pre-application conversation is usually the fastest way to confirm everything lines up.

Pre-application checklist

  • Yield & stress coverage
  • Company vs personal
  • Deposit evidence
  • Credit file
  • Property type
  • Portfolio gearing
  • Exit plan
