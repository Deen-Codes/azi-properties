First-Time Buyer Guide

What costs should first-time buyers plan for besides the deposit?

Saving for a deposit is a major part of buying your first home, but it is not the only cost to consider. First-time buyers should also plan for legal fees, surveys, moving costs and other expenses.

Many first-time buyers focus heavily on the deposit, and rightly so. However, buying a home usually involves several additional costs that can affect your overall budget.

Understanding these costs early can help you prepare more effectively and avoid feeling caught out later in the process.

Legal fees

When buying a property, you will usually need a solicitor or conveyancer to handle the legal side of the purchase. This can include checking contracts, managing searches and helping complete the transaction.

Legal costs can vary, so it is worth factoring them into your budget before you get too far into the buying process.

Survey costs

A survey can help identify potential issues with the property before you buy it. Different types of surveys offer different levels of detail.

While not every buyer chooses the most detailed survey, it can be useful to understand the condition of the property before committing fully.

Mortgage arrangement and valuation fees

Some mortgage products may include arrangement fees, booking fees or valuation-related costs. These depend on the lender and the mortgage product.

A lower interest rate is not always the cheapest option overall if the product carries higher fees, so it is important to consider the full cost.

Stamp Duty considerations

Stamp Duty rules can depend on the purchase price, buyer status and property type. First-time buyers may have different treatment depending on current rules and the value of the property.

Because rules can change, buyers should check the latest position before making decisions.

Moving costs

Moving costs can include removals, van hire, storage, furniture, appliances and setting up your new home.

These smaller costs can add up quickly, especially if you are moving from rented accommodation or furnishing a property for the first time.

Home insurance

Most lenders require buildings insurance to be in place by completion, and many buyers also choose contents insurance to cover the move. These are practical homeownership costs to factor into the wider purchase budget — separate from the mortgage and conveyancing fees.

Emergency savings after completion

It can be helpful to avoid spending every available pound on the purchase. Once you own the property, unexpected repairs or setup costs can appear quickly.

Keeping some savings aside can give you more breathing room after completion.

Final thoughts

First-time buyers should budget for more than the deposit alone. Legal fees, surveys, mortgage-related costs, moving expenses and insurance can all affect the real cost of buying your first home.

Planning ahead can make the process feel more controlled and help you move forward with greater confidence.
