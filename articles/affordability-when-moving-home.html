Moving Home Guide

How does affordability change when buying your next home?

A surprising number of second-time buyers discover their borrowing capacity has shifted in ways they didn't expect. Here's what's actually different the second time round.

When you apply for a mortgage on a new property, lenders start fresh. They don't inherit the affordability assessment from your last mortgage — they do a new one, at today's rates, against today's criteria. What worked before may not work the same way now.

1. Interest rates have moved

Affordability is calculated against a stress-test rate, not just the product rate. If market rates are higher than when you first bought, the same income now supports a smaller loan. This is the single biggest reason second-time buyers find their budget has shrunk.

2. Your commitments have likely grown

Cars on finance, student loans, children's nurseries, credit cards with balances — anything showing as a regular outgoing reduces the income a lender counts toward the mortgage. A clean credit file isn't the same as a clear monthly outgoing picture.

3. Your income picture may have changed

If you've moved from employed to self-employed, become a contractor, started a business, or changed income structure, underwriting becomes more detailed. Lenders look for two to three years of stable self-employed accounts in most cases — though there are specialist lenders with shorter records accepted.

4. Affordability rules have tightened — or loosened

Regulatory and lender criteria shift. In 2022, the Bank of England's 3% stress rate requirement was formally withdrawn, which actually opened up borrowing for many. Other criteria — like how bonuses are counted, how childcare is assessed, or how existing portfolio debt is treated — change year to year.

5. Loan-to-income caps still apply

Most UK lenders are capped at a portfolio level on how much lending above 4.5× income they can do. That means even when your affordability calculation works, the product may not. This is particularly common for applicants near the upper edge of their borrowing range.

6. Joint applications multiply the impact

Most moves involve two applicants. That means two incomes assessed, two credit files pulled, two sets of commitments considered — and if one applicant's situation has changed materially, it can shift the overall picture more than expected.

7. Deposits and equity

The equity from your current home becomes the deposit for the next. A stronger equity position often unlocks better LTV bands, which improves the rate and can loosen the affordability calculation slightly. Selling in a soft market may do the opposite.

What good preparation looks like

  • Pull your credit file before the first application
  • Clear small finance agreements where practical
  • Gather three months of payslips, bank statements and (if self-employed) two to three years of accounts or SA302s
  • Get a realistic, written affordability figure from a broker before you view properties
  • Don't rely on online calculators — they're a starting point, not the answer

Final thoughts

Second-time buyers often assume the mortgage will be the easy part. In practice, lenders apply the same scrutiny — with a different backdrop. Getting an early, accurate picture of what's actually available is the fastest way to avoid a surprise mid-offer.
